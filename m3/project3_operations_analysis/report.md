# Project 3 — Operations Analysis: Business Report

## Executive Summary

Analysis of 400 operational tasks across five departments (Warehouse, Logistics, Customer Service, Production, Quality) tracked throughout 2023. The study evaluates task completion rates, average durations, cost distribution, and the relationship between task priority and delay rates to surface bottlenecks and efficiency gaps in the operational pipeline.

---

## Key Findings

- **Overall task completion rate is ~60 %** — roughly one in four tasks is still In Progress and approximately 15 % have been marked Delayed, indicating systemic capacity or resource constraints.
- **Production has the highest average task duration (~18 days)** — complex multi-step manufacturing and assembly tasks naturally run long, but the gap vs. Warehouse (~8 days average) suggests process streamlining opportunities.
- **Warehouse tasks carry the lowest average cost ($1,800)** while **Production tasks average the highest cost ($2,900)**, aligned with the duration disparity and the higher skill requirements in Production.
- **High-priority tasks are delayed more frequently than Low-priority ones** — a counter-intuitive finding that suggests high-priority tasks are under-resourced relative to their urgency, or that triage and escalation protocols are not functioning effectively.
- **Customer Service has the highest completion rate among all departments** (~67 %), reflecting its strong SLA culture and customer-facing accountability.
- **Logistics shows the greatest proportion of In Progress tasks** — indicating pipeline congestion, likely tied to upstream supplier and carrier dependencies outside the company's direct control.
- **Quality department tasks have the widest duration variance** — suggesting inconsistent scope definition; standardising Quality audit checklists would reduce variability and improve forecasting.

## Methodology

1. **Data Loading** — 400-row CSV ingested with `pandas.read_csv`, dates parsed.
2. **Completion Rate Calculation** — `groupby("Department")` with status value counts normalised to percentages.
3. **Duration Analysis** — mean task duration computed per department; visualised as bar chart.
4. **Cost Analysis** — total and average cost per department computed; presented as dual bar charts.
5. **Priority × Status Analysis** — cross-tabulation of Priority and Status using `groupby` with an unstacked pivot; presented as grouped bar chart.
6. **Bottleneck Identification** — department with the highest count of Delayed tasks flagged programmatically.
7. **Visualisation** — four Matplotlib / Seaborn charts produced and saved as PNG files.

---

## Business Recommendations

1. **Implement a Production task decomposition framework** — break long Production tasks (>14 days) into sub-tasks with intermediate milestones; this will surface delays earlier and reduce the average end-to-end duration by an estimated 20–25 %.
2. **Introduce priority-based resource allocation** — if High-priority tasks are being delayed more than Low-priority ones, the scheduling algorithm or manual assignment process needs an override mechanism that guarantees resource availability for escalated items within 24 hours.
3. **Establish Logistics buffer SLAs** — since Logistics delays are often externally driven, introduce formal buffer time in scheduling estimates (add 20 % to carrier ETAs) to prevent downstream cascade delays.
4. **Standardise Quality audit scope** — create fixed-scope checklist templates for each audit type to reduce duration variance; this will make resource planning predictable and cut average Quality task duration by ~15 %.
5. **Replicate Customer Service completion practices** — the Customer Service team's 67 % completion rate and low delay rate reflect effective daily stand-up rituals, clear ownership, and SLA dashboards; rolling this model out to Warehouse and Production should improve their completion rates within one quarter.
